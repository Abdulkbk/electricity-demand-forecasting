Short-Term Electrical Load Demand Forecasting using Artificial Intelligence Techniques

A Project Proposal by Abdullahi Yunus (U18EE1106)

Project Overview

This section introduces the project, outlining the importance of accurate electrical load forecasting for modern power systems and the potential of Artificial Intelligence (AI) to enhance these predictions, particularly for short-term needs.

1. Introduction

The reliable and efficient operation of modern power systems is critically dependent on accurate electrical load forecasting. Load forecasting predicts future electricity demand, enabling utility companies to make informed decisions regarding power generation scheduling, energy trading, grid stability, and resource management. Inaccuracies in forecasting can lead to increased operational costs, potential energy shortages, or unnecessary surplus generation, all of which have economic and environmental implications.

Short-term load forecasting (STLF), which typically predicts load from a few hours to a week ahead, is particularly crucial for daily operational planning. Traditional forecasting methods, often based on statistical time-series models, can struggle with the increasing complexity and non-linearity of load patterns influenced by diverse factors such as weather conditions, socio-economic activities, and the growing penetration of renewable energy sources and new load types (e.g., electric vehicles).

Artificial Intelligence (AI), particularly Machine Learning (ML) and Deep Learning (DL) techniques, has emerged as a powerful approach to address these challenges. AI models can learn complex patterns from historical data and adapt to changing dynamics, offering the potential for significantly improved forecasting accuracy. This project aims to explore, implement, and evaluate various AI-based models for STLF.

2. Problem Statement

Accurate STLF is a persistent challenge for power system operators. Electrical load series are inherently complex, non-linear, and influenced by a multitude of correlated factors including:

  • Weather conditions: Temperature, humidity, wind speed, and solar irradiance significantly impact heating, ventilation, air conditioning (HVAC), and lighting loads.
  • Temporal variations: Load patterns vary by time of day, day of the week, season, and are affected by public holidays and special events.
  • Stochasticity: Random human behavior and unforeseen events introduce inherent uncertainty.

Traditional forecasting methods may not adequately capture these complex, non-linear relationships. This project seeks to address the need for more robust and accurate STLF models by leveraging the capabilities of AI techniques to better model these influencing factors and improve prediction accuracy.

Nigerian Context: In the context of the Nigerian power grid, additional complexities such as intermittent supply and load shedding present unique challenges that this project may also explore if relevant data becomes accessible.

Aim & Objectives

This section clearly defines the primary goal of the project and breaks it down into specific, measurable objectives that will guide the research and development process.

Aim

To develop and evaluate accurate and robust Artificial Intelligence-based models for short-term electrical load forecasting.

Objectives

  • To conduct a comprehensive literature review of existing STLF techniques, with a focus on AI-based approaches.
  • To acquire and pre-process a suitable historical load dataset along with relevant exogenous variables (e.g., weather data, calendar information).
  • To perform feature engineering and selection to identify the most influential factors for load demand.
  • To implement and train a selection of AI models for STLF, including traditional machine learning algorithms (e.g., Random Forest, Gradient Boosting) and deep learning models (e.g., LSTM).
  • To evaluate the performance of the developed models using appropriate statistical metrics (e.g., RMSE, MAPE, MAE) and compare their effectiveness.
  • To analyze the results, draw conclusions on the suitability of different AI models for STLF, and provide recommendations for future work.

Approach & Scope

Here, the project's boundaries are detailed, including the forecasting timeframe, data sources, AI techniques to be explored, and evaluation methods. The step-by-step methodology for executing the project is also outlined.

4. Scope of Work

Forecasting Horizon:

The project will focus on Short-Term Load Forecasting, specifically predicting hourly load demand for the next 24 to 48 hours.

Dataset:

Initially, this project will utilize a publicly available, well-documented historical load dataset from an established international grid operator (e.g., ISO New England, ENTSO-E Transparency Platform, or a similar dataset from a Kaggle competition). This ensures data quality and availability for focusing on model development.

If a suitable and accessible dataset for a specific region or feeder in Nigeria can be obtained promptly, the scope can be adapted to focus on the local context. This would be a preferred outcome, but pragmatic considerations for data acquisition within the project timeline necessitate starting with a public dataset.

Data Sources and Features:
  • Historical hourly electrical load data (minimum 2-3 years).
  • Corresponding historical hourly weather data (e.g., temperature, humidity, wind speed).
  • Calendar features (e.g., hour of day, day of week, month, holiday indicators).
  • Lagged load values from previous time steps.
AI Techniques to be Explored (Tentative):
  • Baseline model (e.g., SARIMA or a simple persistence model).
  • Machine Learning Models: Random Forest, XGBoost.
  • Deep Learning Models: Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks.
  • Comparison of at least two distinct AI approaches.
Evaluation Metrics:

Model performance will be assessed using Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE), and Mean Absolute Error (MAE).

5. Methodology

The project will be executed in the following phases:

  1. Literature Review: A thorough review of academic papers, journals, and conference proceedings on STLF, machine learning, and deep learning applications in power systems.
  2. Data Acquisition and Understanding: Selection and download of a suitable public dataset. Initial exploration to understand its characteristics.
  3. Data Pre-processing: Handling missing values, data cleaning, outlier detection, normalization/scaling, and splitting data into training, validation, and testing sets.
  4. Feature Engineering and Selection: Creation of relevant features and selection of the most influential ones.
  5. Model Development and Training: Implementation of selected AI models, hyperparameter tuning, and training.
  6. Model Evaluation and Comparison: Evaluating models on the test dataset and comparing their performance.
  7. Analysis, Documentation, and Reporting: Interpreting results, documenting the process, writing the final report, and preparing a presentation.
